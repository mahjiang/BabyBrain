# Baby Carrier / Wrap

> **Status:** Researching | **Buy by:** Week 32-34 | **Used OK:** Yes, if structurally sound

## Why It Matters for Us

Baby wearing is especially useful for apartment + transit life:
- Hands-free at home and on the go
- Easier on transit than a stroller
- Helps with bonding and calming fussy babies
- Takes up almost no storage space

## Types

| Type | Ages | Pros | Cons |
|------|------|------|------|
| **Wrap** (Solly, Boba) | Newborn–~25 lbs | Cozy, newborn-friendly, cheap | Learning curve to tie |
| **Ring sling** | Newborn–~35 lbs | Quick on/off, nursing-friendly | One-shoulder weight |
| **Soft structured carrier** (Ergobaby, Lillebaby) | Newborn–toddler | Easy to use, distributes weight well | Bulkier, pricier |
| **Mei tai** | ~3 months–toddler | Adjustable, good weight distribution | Less common, learning curve |

### Safety Framework: TICKS Guidelines

*Evidence (consensus guidance from babywearing organizations and IHDI):*

The TICKS rule is the standard safety checklist for any carrier type:

- **T — Tight:** Carrier should be snug with no loose fabric; baby held high on body with head support close enough to kiss
- **I — In view at all times:** Always able to see baby's face by glancing down; never obscured by fabric
- **C — Close enough to kiss:** Baby's head close enough that you can easily kiss their forehead
- **K — Keep chin off chest:** Baby's chin should never be resting on their chest (airway obstruction risk)
- **S — Supported back:** Baby's back supported in natural curved position; not slumped

(Source: [TICKS Babywearing Safety Rules](http://wiki.babywearingdiy.com/do/view/Main/TicksRules))

### Hip Health: The M-Position Standard

*Evidence (International Hip Dysplasia Institute):*

Healthy carrier positioning places baby's hips in the "M" or "frog" position:
- Knees bent, higher than bottom
- Hips and knees bent, knees falling out to the sides
- Carrier supports under the thigh from knee to hip — not just the bottom

**The opposite position** — legs dangling straight down, held together — increases risk of hip dysplasia.

The IHDI recommends **inward-facing carry for the first 6 months** as most ergonomic for hip development.

Carriers explicitly certified as hip-healthy by IHDI include: Beco Gemini, Solly Baby Wrap, and others that support the M-position. The Lillebaby's newborn "fetal position" (legs tucked inside the carrier) is NOT IHDI-recommended — legs should be out in the M-position.

(Source: [International Hip Dysplasia Institute — Baby Wearing](https://hipdysplasia.org/baby-wearing/), [IHDI — Baby Carriers](https://hipdysplasia.org/baby-carriers-other-equipment/))

*Evidence (BabyGearLab testing, manufacturer specifications; note: BGL has affiliate links):*

### Applying the Oster Framework: Is Babywearing "Safe"?

*Evidence (IHDI, TICKS guidelines, clinical consensus):*

There is no controversy about babywearing safety when done correctly. The risk factors are:
1. **Airway obstruction** — addressed by the TICKS rules (chin off chest, face visible, close enough to kiss)
2. **Hip dysplasia** — addressed by the M-position / IHDI certification
3. **Falls** — carrier must be properly secured; never babywear while doing activities that risk falling

The question is not "is babywearing safe?" but "am I following the positioning guidelines?" — that's answered by using an IHDI-certified carrier and following TICKS.

*What Oster would say:* The absolute risk of harm from babywearing is very low when using a well-designed carrier with proper positioning. The real failure mode is improper airway positioning, which is preventable with the TICKS checklist. The benefits (hands-free mobility, bonding, transit flexibility) are concrete and practical. This is a low-evidence-needed decision — buy a good carrier and learn proper positioning.

### The Two-Carrier Strategy for Our Situation

*Opinion (Eric's synthesis):*

Many transit-focused parents end up with two carriers because no single carrier is optimal for all phases:

1. **Newborn phase (0–4 months):** Stretchy wrap (Solly Baby, ~$74) — best for skin-to-skin contact at home and early transit trips; lightweight; maximum coziness for newborn
2. **Ongoing carry (4 months+):** Soft structured carrier (Ergobaby Omni or Beco Gemini, ~$110–$179) — practical for all positions, back carry when older, long transit days

Total cost: ~$185–$253 for both. Wrap is a strong registry or used purchase.

Alternatively, **skip the wrap** and start with the SSC from birth — the Ergobaby Omni 360 and Beco Gemini are both rated for 7 lb newborns without an insert. Simpler and cheaper if the learning curve of wrapping is a concern.

A **ring sling** (WildBird, ~$55–$80) is worth having as a third tool if transit use is frequent — best quick-on/off experience for hopping on/off trains.
